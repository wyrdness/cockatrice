## Overview

The cockatrice is a legendary creature of medieval European bestiary tradition, conventionally depicted as a two-legged dragon or wyvern-like beast with the head, comb, wattles, and feet of a rooster (cock) and the long, scaled, serpentine tail of a snake or dragon. Its supposed powers include a deadly stare that kills or petrifies, poisonous breath, and a lethal touch — abilities largely shared with, and often identical to, those of the basilisk, with which the cockatrice is frequently conflated in late-medieval English usage. The creature is said to hatch from a yolkless ('cock's') egg laid by a seven-year-old rooster and incubated by a toad or serpent on a dunghill. Its weaknesses, according to medieval lore, include the crow of a rooster (which kills it instantly), the gaze of a weasel (the only animal immune to its own gaze), and its own reflection in a mirror. The English word 'cockatrice' was popularised by John Wycliffe's 1382 Bible translation, which used it to render Hebrew terms for venomous serpents in the Book of Isaiah and elsewhere. From the late 14th century onward the cockatrice appears in heraldry, in works of literature including Edmund Spenser's Faerie Queene and Shakespeare's Richard III and Romeo and Juliet, and in modern fantasy fiction. While zoologically a fiction, the figure is a long-attested feature of European cultural and religious history and remains a recognisable emblem of medieval monstrology.

## Etymology

- **Origin**: From Old French cocatris, ultimately from Medieval Latin calcatrix ('tracker'), itself a translation of Greek ichneumon ('tracker'), the Egyptian mongoose. The English word entered through Anglo-Norman in the 14th century.
- **Meaning**: Originally 'tracker' (referring to the mongoose's role in folklore as a hunter of crocodiles); by transfer in medieval Latin and Old French, the term came to denote the legendary basilisk-like beast.
- **First use**: Earliest English-language attestation is in John Wycliffe's Bible translation of 1382.

## Theories & Explanations

### Paranormal / Cryptozoological

**Bestiary 'real creature' tradition** _(Alexander Neckam, Bartholomeus Anglicus, Edward Topsell)_

Medieval bestiary writers treated the cockatrice (and basilisk) as a genuine, if extremely rare, animal of waste places, alongside lions and elephants. The figure carried allegorical-Christian meaning as a symbol of sin and the devil.

### Skeptical

**Mistranslation hypothesis** _(Modern biblical translators (RSV, NIV editorial committees))_

Modern biblical scholarship treats Wycliffe's 'cockatrice' as a mistranslation of Hebrew names for ordinary venomous snakes (e.g. sap'on, 'efeh); the cockatrice's expansion in English bestiaries is a literary rather than zoological development.

**Yolkless-egg explanation** _(Modern poultry biologists, Folklorists of European zoology)_

Modern biology accounts for occasional 'cock's eggs' (small, yolkless eggs sometimes laid by young or stressed hens) as the natural anomaly that originally inspired the cockatrice incubation legend.

**Snake aposematism / horned-viper hypothesis** _(Comparative folklorists of the Mediterranean)_

Some folklorists argue the basilisk-cockatrice complex began as exaggerated reports of venomous snakes (e.g., the horned viper Cerastes cerastes), with the rooster's-head iconography accreted later.
